## **S**

### **Shell–Centre Duality**

**Definition**  

The structural duality between energy located on the shell of the distribution sphere and energy located at the centre.  
It distinguishes light‑like behaviour (shell) from mass‑like behaviour (centre).

**Conceptual Role**  

This duality expresses:

- light on the shell (radius = 1)  
- mass at the centre (radius = 0)  
- the difference between propagation and localisation  
- the mapping between causal energy distribution and phenomenal appearance  
- the geometric origin of inertia vs free propagation  

A point on the shell corresponds to a mode with no rest mass.  
A point at the centre corresponds to a mode with no spatial extension.

**Geometric Expression**  

On the x‑sphere:

- \(r = 1\): shell (light)  
- \(r = 0\): centre (mass)

**Cross‑links**  

Radial Coordinate, x‑sphere, k‑sphere, Ribbon, Mass/Light Distinction, Energy Distribution Sphere.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Shell–Centre Duality is the geometric distinction between light and mass.  
Light lives on the shell; mass lives at the centre.  
This simple radial structure underlies propagation, localisation, and the behaviour of energy in the OP‑framework.



### **Spatial Distribution Sphere (x‑sphere)**

**Definition**  

The sphere in the phenomenal domain representing the spatial distribution of the ortho‑pair fields.  
It is the projection of the causal energy distribution into observable space.

**Conceptual Role**  

The x‑sphere expresses:

- the spatial footprint of the ribbon  
- the shell–centre structure (light vs mass)  
- the observable oscillation pattern  
- the mapping from k‑domain rotation to x‑domain appearance  
- the geometry used by the Fourier transform  

It is the phenomenal counterpart of the k‑sphere.

**Geometric Expression**  

A unit sphere in the phenomenal domain:
$$
\|\vec{x}\| = 1
$$

**Cross‑links**  

k‑sphere, Shell–Centre Duality, Radial Coordinate, Ortho‑Pairs, Ribbon, FT.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The spatial distribution sphere is the phenomenal‑domain sphere on which the ribbon’s oscillation appears.  
It encodes the observable structure of the layer and mirrors the causal geometry of the k‑sphere.


### **Spherical Coordinates**

**Definition**  

A coordinate system on the sphere defined by radial distance, polar angle, and azimuthal angle.  
Used to describe positions on the x‑sphere and k‑sphere.

**Conceptual Role**  

Spherical coordinates provide:

- a natural description of the dual spheres  
- angular coordinates for Q‑angle  
- radial coordinates for shell–centre structure  
- a clean mapping between causal and phenomenal domains  

They are the native coordinates of the OP‑framework.

**Geometric Expression**  
$$
(r, \theta, \phi)
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Radial Coordinate, Q‑angle, Orientation Sphere, x‑sphere, k‑sphere.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Spherical coordinates are the natural language of the OP‑geometry.  
They describe the dual spheres and provide the angular and radial structure used throughout the model.

### **Spin (Ribbon Handedness)**

**Definition**  

The handedness of the ribbon’s helical rotation.  
It is the geometric analogue of quantum spin, arising from the direction of phase rotation around the Q‑axis.

**Conceptual Role**  

Spin expresses:

- the chirality of the ribbon  
- the direction of phase rotation  
- the handedness of the helical structure  
- the sign of the ortho‑pair coupling  
- the orientation of the causal rotation relative to the observer  

Spin is not an independent quantum number; it is a geometric property of the ribbon.

**Geometric Expression**  

Right‑handed or left‑handed rotation around the Q‑axis:
$$
\varphi(t) \to \pm \varphi(t)
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Ribbon, Phase Rotation, Q‑axis, Ortho‑Pairs, Polarisation.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Spin is the handedness of the ribbon’s rotation.  
It arises naturally from the geometry and replaces the abstract quantum notion with a concrete rotational structure.



### **SU(3) Colour Layer** *(optional)*

**Definition**  

An optional OP‑layer whose internal symmetry is SU(3).  

It represents a three‑mode phase structure on a single phase circle, with three preferred phase directions separated by 120°.  
This layer is included only if the manuscript uses colour‑space analogies or multi‑component internal states.

**Conceptual Role**  

If retained, the SU(3) layer provides:

- a three‑mode generalisation of the phase circle  
- a natural home for triadic internal states  
- a geometric interpretation of “colour” as three phase directions  
- a closure rule where the three energy components sum to neutrality  

It is not required for the core OP‑stack but can be used to illustrate multi‑phase interactions.

**Geometric Expression**  

A symmetry acting on a complex 3‑component internal state, represented geometrically as three phase directions on a single circle, separated by \(120^\circ\).

**Cross‑links**  

Phase Rotation, Ribbon Geometry (extended), Symmetry Breaking, Multi‑Phase Modes.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The SU(3) colour layer is an optional extension that introduces a three‑component internal symmetry.  
It models systems with three coupled phase modes and provides a geometric analogue of colour charge without requiring full particle‑physics machinery.



### **Symmetry Breaking**

**Definition**  

A transition in which a previously symmetric configuration selects a specific orientation, axis, or phase.  
In the OP‑framework, it occurs when a layer chooses a Q‑axis or phase alignment.

**Conceptual Role**  

Symmetry breaking explains:

- why a layer selects a specific Q‑axis  
- how canonical frames emerge  
- why polarisation has a definite tilt  
- how mass and light modes differentiate  
- how layers acquire distinct identities  

It is the geometric origin of structure.

**Geometric Expression**  

Selection of a specific axis from a symmetric set:
$$
Q = \text{chosen axis from } S^2
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Q‑axis, Polarisation, Phase Plane, Ribbon, Canonical Axes.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Symmetry breaking is the moment a layer chooses its Q‑axis or phase orientation.  
It turns a symmetric potential into a structured geometry and gives the layer its identity.
