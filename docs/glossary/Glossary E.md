### **Energy Distribution Sphere (k‑sphere)**

**Definition**

The 3‑sphere representing the distribution of energy or momentum for the child layer.  
It is the Fourier‑dual of the spatial distribution sphere (x‑sphere).

**Conceptual Role**

The k‑sphere encodes how the child’s energy is spread across momentum space.  
It is the target domain of the layer‑local Fourier transform and expresses the energetic structure of the layer once the geometry has been mapped out of the x‑domain.

**Geometric Expression**

A hypersphere in the k‑domain:
$$
S_k^3 = \{\, \mathbf{k} \in \mathbb{R}^4 : |\mathbf{k}| = \text{constant} \,\}
$$

**Cross‑links**

Spatial Distribution Sphere, FT, Dual Hyperspheres, Canonical Axes.

**Interpretive Summary**

The k‑sphere represents the child layer’s energy distribution and is the Fourier‑dual of the spatial x‑sphere.  
Together, the two spheres form the dual‑hypersphere structure that underlies the layer‑local FT.



### **Energy Ribbon**

**Definition**

A representation of the photon as a spiralling ribbon in spacetime, where the ribbon’s twist encodes phase rotation and its pitch encodes energy.

**Conceptual Role**

The energy ribbon is an intuitive geometric metaphor for how phase rotation generates the intrinsic Q‑axis \(Q_c\), how precession emerges, and how energy is expressed as twist density.  
It is optional but pedagogically powerful, especially when introducing the photon’s internal geometry.

**Geometric Expression**

A helical strip embedded in spacetime, with twist rate proportional to phase frequency:
$$
\text{twist rate} \;\propto\; \omega
$$

**Cross‑links**

Phase Plane, Ribbon Geometry, \(Q_c\), Precession.

**Interpretive Summary**

The energy ribbon visualises the photon as a spiralling strip whose twist encodes phase and energy.  
As the ribbon rotates, it generates the phase plane and its normal \(Q_c\), making the ribbon a vivid geometric analogue for the internal structure of a massless layer.

### **Extrinsic Geometry (Observer Frame)**

**Definition**

The geometry defined relative to the inherited parent Q‑axis \(Q_p\).  
It represents how the child layer appears from the parent’s perspective before alignment.

**Conceptual Role**

Extrinsic geometry determines the observed polarisation tilt and the apparent orientation of the child’s phase plane.  
It is the frame that must be rotated (via alignment) to bring \(Q_p\) into coincidence with the child’s intrinsic axis \(Q_c\), ensuring that the FT sees a clean canonical configuration.

**Geometric Expression**

A coordinate system based on:
$$
(x_p,\ k_p,\ Q_p)
$$
before alignment or axis conversion.

**Cross‑links**

\(Q_p\), Polarisation, Alignment, Axis Conversion, Observer Frame.

**Interpretive Summary**

Extrinsic geometry is the observer’s view of the child layer, defined by the inherited parent Q‑axis.  
It encodes the observer‑dependent distortions — such as polarisation tilt — that must be removed before the child’s intrinsic geometry can be analysed or transformed.
