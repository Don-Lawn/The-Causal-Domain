## **T**

### **Tempo (dt/dT)**

**Definition**  

The ratio between phenomenal time \(t\) and causal time \(T\).  
Tempo measures how fast phenomenal time advances relative to the underlying causal rotation of the ribbon.

**Conceptual Role**  

Tempo replaces “time dilation” with a geometric quantity derived from the ribbon’s pitch.  
It expresses:

- how quickly the ribbon advances in Q‑depth per unit causal rotation  
- the effective rate of phenomenal time  
- the coupling between rotation and vertical progression  
- the geometric origin of relativistic effects  

A higher Tempo means phenomenal time advances quickly;  
a lower Tempo means phenomenal time advances slowly.

Tempo is not imposed — it **emerges** from the ribbon’s geometry.

**Geometric Expression**  

Tempo is the derivative of phenomenal time with respect to causal time:
$$
\text{Tempo} = \frac{dt}{dT}
$$

Equivalently, it is proportional to ribbon pitch:
$$
\text{Tempo} \propto \text{pitch}
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Ribbon Pitch, Ribbon Frequency, Relativity as Rotation, Q‑depth, Phase Rotation, Projection.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Tempo is the speed of phenomenal time.  
It arises from the ribbon’s helical geometry and replaces time dilation with a clean geometric ratio between causal rotation and phenomenal progression.

### **Temporal Domain (Causal vs Phenomenal)**

**Definition**  

The distinction between causal time \(T\), which drives phase rotation, and phenomenal time \(t\), which emerges from ribbon pitch and Tempo.

**Conceptual Role**  

This duality explains:

- why time is not fundamental  
- how phenomenal time emerges from geometry  
- why Tempo varies between layers  
- how relativistic effects arise from projection  

Causal time is uniform;  
phenomenal time is derived.

**Geometric Expression**  
$$
t(T) = \int \text{Tempo}(T)\, dT
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Tempo, Ribbon Pitch, Phase Rotation, Relativity as Rotation.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The OP‑framework distinguishes between causal time (the driver of rotation) and phenomenal time (the projection of that rotation).  
Tempo links the two.

**Definition**  

The angular misalignment between the inherited parent Q‑axis \(Q_p\) and the child’s intrinsic Q‑axis \(Q_c\).  
It measures how far the child’s canonical frame is rotated relative to the observer.

**Conceptual Role**  

Tilt expresses:

- relative motion between frames  
- the geometric origin of relativistic effects  
- the degree of polarisation  
- the axis around which precession occurs  
- the misalignment that must be corrected before applying the FT  

Tilt is the OP‑framework’s replacement for “relative velocity”.

**Geometric Expression**  
$$
\alpha = \arccos(Q_p \cdot Q_c)
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Q‑axis, \(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Polarisation, Precession, Relativity as Rotation, Alignment.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Tilt is the polarisation angle between inherited and intrinsic Q‑axes.  
It encodes relative motion as geometry rather than velocity, and it drives precession and relativistic projection effects.

### **Transform (Fourier / Hankel)**

**Definition**  

The mathematical projection that maps causal rotation into phenomenal oscillation.  
In Cartesian geometry this is the Fourier transform; in radial geometry it becomes the Hankel transform.

**Conceptual Role**  

The transform expresses:

- rotation–oscillation duality  
- the mapping between k‑domain rotation and x‑domain appearance  
- the projection of the ribbon onto the spatial distribution sphere  
- the decomposition of signals into rotational modes  

The FT is used when the geometry is Cartesian;  
the Hankel transform is used when the geometry is radial.

**Geometric Expression**  

Cartesian:
$$
\tilde{f}(k) = \int f(x)e^{-ikx}\,dx
$$

Radial:
$$
\tilde{f}(k) = \int_0^\infty f(r)J_0(kr)\,r\,dr
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Rotation–Oscillation Duality, Ribbon, Hankel Transform, Projection, x/k Duality.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The transform is the mathematical bridge between causal rotation and phenomenal oscillation.  
It turns the ribbon’s internal rotation into observable wave behaviour.
