## **C**

### **Canonical Axes**

**Definition**

 The intrinsic Q‑axis generated by the child layer’s own phase rotation. It is the normal vector to the child’s phase plane.

**Conceptual Role**

\(Q_c\) defines the child’s internal geometry. It is the axis around which precession occurs, the axis orthogonal to the phase plane, and the axis used by the Fourier transform once alignment has been performed. Unlike the inherited \(Q_p,\) the child’s \(Q_c\) is self‑generated and layer‑specific.

**Geometric Expression**

The normal to the phase plane:
$$
Q_c=\hat {n}_{\mathrm{phase\  plane}}
$$
**Cross‑links**

 \(Q_p\), Phase Plane, Polarisation, Precession, FT.

**Interpretive Summary**

 The child’s \(Q_c\) is its own vertical direction — the axis that emerges naturally from the orientation of its phase plane. While the parent supplies \(Q_p\) as the inherited frame, the child establishes \(Q_c\) through its internal rotation, giving each layer a unique geometric identity. Once alignment brings Q_p into coincidence with Q_c, this intrinsic axis becomes the canonical vertical direction for the Fourier transform and for all of the child’s internal dynamics.

The causal domain is the underlying rotational and energetic geometry that produces the phenomena observed in the child layer.

### **Circle of Observers**

**Definition**  

The circle formed by all possible observer orientations around the child’s phase circle. Each observer corresponds to a unique angular position representing their directional relationship to the phase rotation.

**Conceptual Role**  

The circle of observers expresses relativity as a purely angular phenomenon. Differences between observers—frequency shift, Doppler, simultaneity slicing, polarisation interpretation—are represented as angular separations around the same underlying phase geometry. It unifies observer‑dependence with phase structure.

**Geometric Expression**  

A mapping from observer orientation to phase angle:
$$
\text{Observer}(\theta)\ \longleftrightarrow\ \text{Phase Angle } \theta
$$
where \(\theta\) is the observer’s position around the phase circle.

**Cross‑links**  

Phase Circle, Polarisation, \(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Relativity as Rotation, Observer Frame.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The circle of observers treats each observer as nothing more than a direction on the phase circle. When position is discarded and only direction is kept, every observer becomes an angle \(\theta\) around the same geometric object. Their differences—what they measure, how they slice time, how they interpret polarisation—are simply angular offsets. This makes relativity a rotational effect rather than a translational one: observers differ not by where they are, but by how they are oriented around the phase circle.



### **Cross‑Domain Duality**

**Definition**  

The structural relationship between the spatial domain \(x\) and the energy domain \(k\), in which each domain is the Fourier‑dual of the other. A transformation in one domain corresponds to an orthogonal transformation in the other.

**Conceptual Role**  

Cross‑domain duality explains why localisation in the \(x\)-domain corresponds to spread in the \(k\)-domain, why oscillation in one domain appears as translation in the other, and why the same geometry can be expressed in two complementary coordinate systems. It is the backbone of the OP‑stack’s two‑sphere architecture.

**Geometric Expression**  

A Fourier dual mapping:
$$
x \;\longleftrightarrow\; k
$$
with the transform exchanging:

- localisation \(\leftrightarrow\) spread  
- convolution \(\leftrightarrow\) multiplication  
- phase rotation \(\leftrightarrow\) translation  

**Cross‑links**  

Dual Hyperspheres, FT, Canonical Axes, \(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Spatial Domain, Energy Domain.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Cross‑domain duality is the deep symmetry that lets the same physical structure be described in two different ways. In the \(x\)-domain, geometry appears as shape and localisation; in the \(k\)-domain, the same geometry appears as frequency and energy distribution. The Fourier transform bridges these views, turning spatial structure into energetic structure and vice versa. This duality is not optional or decorative — it is the fundamental reason the OP‑stack can express the same layer in two complementary hyperspheres without contradiction.

### **Cycle (Phase Cycle)**

**Definition**  

A complete rotation of the phase vector around the phase circle, corresponding to a \(2\pi\) change in phase angle. It represents one full oscillatory period of the layer’s internal dynamics.

**Conceptual Role**  

The phase cycle is the fundamental unit of temporal structure within a layer. Every oscillation, wave, or periodic behaviour is built from repeated phase cycles. It defines the natural “tick” of the layer’s internal clock and provides the basis for frequency, Tempo, and phase relationships between layers.

**Geometric Expression**  

A full rotation:
$$
\phi \;\longrightarrow\; \phi + 2\pi
$$
with the phase vector returning to its original orientation:
$$
\vec{v}(\phi + 2\pi) = \vec{v}(\phi)
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Phase Circle, Phase Angle \(\phi\), Tempo \(dt/dT\), Frequency, Oscillation, **Quantisation (Free Space)**.

**Interpretive Summary**  

A phase cycle is the simplest expression of periodicity. As the phase vector completes a full rotation, the layer experiences one unit of internal time — a natural period defined by its geometry rather than by external clocks. This makes the phase cycle the bridge between geometry and temporal behaviour: frequency counts how many cycles occur per unit Tempo, while phase relationships describe how different layers align or misalign their cycles. In free space, only whole cycles propagate coherently, making the phase cycle the atomic unit of quantisation.
