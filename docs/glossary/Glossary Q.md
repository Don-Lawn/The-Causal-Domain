## **Q**

### **Q‑axis**

**Definition**  

A unit vector representing the vertical direction of a layer’s geometry.  
It is the normal to the phase plane and defines the canonical orientation of the layer.

**Conceptual Role**  

The Q‑axis is the backbone of the OP‑layer.  

It determines:

- the orientation of the phase plane  
- the vertical direction for the Fourier transform  
- the axis around which precession is measured  
- the relationship between inherited and intrinsic geometry  

Every layer contains **two** Q‑axes:

- the inherited **parent Q‑axis** \(Q_p\)  
- the self‑generated **child Q‑axis** \(Q_c\)

Their relationship encodes polarisation, precession, and the need for alignment.

**Geometric Expression**  

A unit normal to the phase plane:
$$
Q = \hat{n}_{\text{phase plane}}
$$

**Cross‑links**  

\(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Phase Plane, Polarisation, Precession, Alignment, Canonical Axes.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The Q‑axis defines the vertical direction of a layer’s geometry.  
Its inherited and intrinsic forms structure the entire OP‑stack, from orientation to precession to the canonical frame required by the FT.

### **Dual Q‑axes \((Q_p, Q_c)\)**

**Definition**
The pair of Q‑axes present in every layer:

- \(Q_p\) — the inherited parent Q‑axis (observer frame)
- \(Q_c\) — the child’s self‑generated Q‑axis (intrinsic frame)

**Conceptual Role**

The tilt between \(Q_p\) and \(Q_c\) defines **polarisation**.
The rotation of \(Q_c\) around \(Q_p\) defines **precession** (mass).
Alignment brings \(Q_p\) into coincidence with \(Q_c\).

**Geometric Expression**

Two axes in the orientation sphere:
$$
\angle(Q_p, Q_c) = \text{polarisation tilt}
$$

**Cross‑links**

Polarisation, Precession, Alignment, \(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Observer Frame.

**Interpretive Summary**

Every layer contains two Q‑axes: the inherited observer axis and the intrinsic axis generated by the child’s own phase rotation. Their relationship encodes polarisation, precession, and the need for alignment before applying the FT.



### **Q‑depth**

**Definition**  

A scalar coordinate measuring position along the Q‑axis.  
It represents how far a point lies above or below the phase plane along the axis.

**Conceptual Role**  

Q‑depth provides a linear coordinate for describing:

- vertical displacement  
- shell vs centre structure  
- mass distribution  
- projection of precessional motion  

It complements angular coordinates on the orientation sphere.

**Geometric Expression**  

Projection onto the Q‑axis:
$$
d_Q = \vec{r} \cdot Q
$$

**Cross‑links**  

Q‑axis, Q‑angle/Depth Duality, Orientation Sphere, Precession.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Q‑depth is the linear coordinate along the Q‑axis.  
It expresses vertical structure in the layer and pairs naturally with angular coordinates on the orientation sphere.

### **Q‑angle / Q‑depth Duality**

**Definition**  

The structural duality between angular coordinates on the orientation sphere and linear coordinates along the Q‑axis.  
It expresses two complementary ways of describing orientation and vertical position.

**Conceptual Role**  

This duality explains how:

- rotation around the sphere corresponds to depth along the axis  
- angular precession maps to vertical displacement  
- the same geometry can be expressed in spherical or axial form  

It mirrors the deeper dualities in the OP‑framework (x/k, phase/energy).

**Geometric Expression**  

Two coordinate systems on the same monorail:

- angular coordinate \(\theta\) on the orientation sphere  
- linear coordinate \(d_Q\) along the Q‑axis  

**Cross‑links**  

Q‑axis, Q‑depth, Orientation Sphere, Polarisation, Precession.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Q‑angle and Q‑depth are two ways of describing the same geometric structure.  
One is angular, one is linear; together they provide a complete coordinate system for orientation and vertical position.
