# **1  Diffraction as Boundary‑Driven Rotation**

Motion and gravity showed that massive systems in the **Spatial Domain** change their behaviour when their Q‑orientation changes.
 Electromagnetic fields obey the same geometric rule.
 A boundary does not merely redirect a wave; it **forces a rotation of the field’s Q‑Orientation Vector**, and the projection of that rotation into the Spatial Domain produces spreading, bending, interference, and mode formation.

Diffraction is therefore not a mysterious wave phenomenon.
 It is the first clear sign that the same Q‑geometry acting on matter also acts on fields — but through a different orthogonal pair.



### **1.1  Boundaries Force Rotation**

A slit, edge, or conducting surface restricts the electromagnetic field.
 Restriction increases the rotation angle \theta  of the field’s Q‑Orientation Vector.

As \theta  increases:

- the field’s **Tempo in the Spatial Domain decreases**,
- the **projection** of its advance along the propagation axis shortens,
- and the field reorganises to satisfy the boundary condition.

Nothing inside the field “wiggles” differently.
 Its internal Tempo — the Tempo in its own Energy Domain representation — remains unchanged.
 But from the outside, the **Spatial Domain projection** of its advance is altered by the boundary‑induced rotation.

This is the electromagnetic analogue of the Lorentz circle:
 a rotation in Q produces a change in projection, not a change in internal dynamics.

### **1.2  Projection Becomes Spreading**

When the projection of a field’s advance is reduced, its apparent behaviour changes:

- the beam spreads,
- the wavefront bends,
- interference fringes appear,
- and the field reorganises into discrete modes.

These are not intrinsic wave distortions.
 They are **projection effects** caused by a rotation of the Q‑Orientation Vector enforced by the boundary.

A narrow slit forces a large rotation.
 A wide aperture forces a small one.
 A perfect conductor forces \theta \rightarrow 90^{\circ }, extinguishing Tempo along the forbidden direction.

The geometry is the same as in motion and gravity.
 Only the physical system changes.



### **1.3  Diffraction as the Clue to the Second Ortho‑Pair**

Diffraction does not *form* the Second Ortho‑Pair.
 It **reveals** it.

Motion and gravity showed that worldlines of massive systems rotate into Q.
 Boundaries now show that **fields** rotate into Q as well — but in a way that exposes a deeper structure.

When a boundary forces a rotation into Q, the electromagnetic field reorganises into spreading beams, bent wavefronts, and quantised modes.
 This behaviour is not arbitrary.
 It is the shadow of a rotation acting on the two perpendicular components of the field:

- **E** — the electric component
- **B** — the magnetic component

These are not separate entities.
 They are the two perpendicular legs of the **Second Ortho‑Pair**:
 two axes of a single rotation in Q‑space that governs field behaviour.

Diffraction is the observational clue that the same rotational geometry now acts on **fields** instead of **matter**.

Thus:

- **RSR ⟂ RGR** form the First Ortho‑Pair (massive systems, Spatial Domain)
- **E ⟂ B** form the Second Ortho‑Pair (fields, Energy Domain)



### **1.4  Locating the Two Ortho‑Pairs in Q‑Space**

The First Ortho‑Pair belongs to the **Spatial Domain**.
 It lives on the **Q = 0 shell**, where worldlines of massive systems reside.
 Motion and gravity act here.
 Their rotations tilt worldlines into Q, but the systems themselves remain anchored to the Spatial Domain.

The Second Ortho‑Pair belongs to the **Energy Domain**.
 It lives on the **Q = 1 shell**, the non‑material layer where electromagnetic fields exist.
 Here, the orthogonal pair is not (motion ⟂ gravity) but **(E ⟂ B)** — the two perpendicular components of the electromagnetic field.
 Their rotations occur entirely within the field layer, independent of mass.

Diffraction is the clue that reveals this structure.
 A boundary forces a rotation into Q, and the projection of that rotation alters the balance between E and B.
 The spreading of a beam, the bending of a wavefront, and the quantisation of modes are all shadows of this deeper geometry.

Thus:

**First Ortho‑Pair (RSR ⟂ RGR)**

- domain: **Spatial Domain (x‑domain)**
- location: **Q = 0**
- behaviour: worldlines, motion, gravity

**Second Ortho‑Pair (E ⟂ B)**

- domain: **Energy Domain (k‑domain)**
- location: **Q = 1**
- behaviour: waves, phase, quantisation

The two shells are parallel layers of the same geometry.
 The same rotation mechanism acts in both, but the actors differ:
 matter on the inner shell, fields on the outer.
