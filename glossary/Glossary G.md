## G

### **Gauge Freedom (Phase Gauge)**

**Definition**

The freedom to choose the zero‑point of phase rotation without changing the physical state of the child layer.  
A shift in phase origin does not alter \(Q_c\), precession, or the geometry of the hyperspheres.

**Conceptual Role**

Gauge freedom ensures that the phase angle is not an absolute quantity.  
Only *differences* in phase matter, and only the *orientation* of the phase plane affects Q‑geometry.  
This removes any physical significance from the choice of where \(\phi = 0\) is placed.

**Geometric Expression**

A shift:
$$
\phi \;\rightarrow\; \phi + \phi_0
$$
with no change to the phase plane’s normal vector \(Q_c\).

**Cross‑links**

Phase Rotation, Phase Plane, \(Q_c\), Canonical Axes.

**Interpretive Summary**

Gauge freedom reflects the fact that the phase origin is arbitrary.  
Changing the zero‑point of phase does not affect the geometry, the Q‑axis, or any physical observable.  
Only the *orientation* of the phase plane — not the absolute phase value — carries geometric meaning.



### **Geometry of the Phase Plane**

**Definition**

The 2D plane in which the child’s phase rotation occurs.  
Its orientation determines the direction of the child’s intrinsic Q‑axis \(Q_c\).

**Conceptual Role**

The phase plane is the geometric generator of \(Q_c\).  
Its tilt relative to the inherited \(Q_p\) defines **polarisation**,  
and its precession around \(Q_p\) defines **mass**.  
Everything the child layer becomes — its axis, its hyperspheres, its FT frame — originates from the orientation of this plane.

**Geometric Expression**

A plane spanned by two orthonormal vectors \((e_1, e_2)\) with normal vector:
$$
Q_c = e_1 \times e_2
$$

**Cross‑links**

Phase Rotation, \(Q_c\), Polarisation, Precession, Canonical Axes.

**Interpretive Summary**

The phase plane is the rotating 2D surface whose orientation defines the child’s Q‑axis and whose dynamics encode polarisation and mass.  
It is the foundational geometric object of the layer: rotate the plane, and you rotate \(Q_c\); precess the plane, and you generate mass; align the plane, and you prepare the layer for the FT.

### **Global vs Local Axes**

**Definition**

The distinction between the axes inherited from the parent layer (global, extrinsic) and the axes generated by the child layer (local, intrinsic).

**Conceptual Role**

Global axes define the observer frame through the inherited parent Q‑axis \(Q_p\).
Local axes define the child’s internal geometry through its self‑generated Q‑axis \(Q_c\).
Alignment reconciles the two by rotating the observer frame so that \(Q_p\) coincides with \(Q_c\).

**Geometric Expression**

Global axes:
$$
(x_p,\ k_p,\ Q_p)
$$

Local axes:
$$
(x_c,\ k_c,\ Q_c)
$$

**Cross‑links**  

\(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Alignment, Axis Conversion, Observer Frame.

**Interpretive Summary**  

Global axes describe the observer’s inherited frame; local axes describe the child’s intrinsic geometry.  
The difference between them encodes polarisation tilt, and alignment removes that difference so the child’s geometry can be expressed canonically for the FT.
