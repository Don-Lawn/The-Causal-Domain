## **O**

### **Observer Frame**

**Definition**  

The coordinate frame defined by the inherited Q‑axis \(Q_p\).  
It is the parent’s perspective on the child layer before alignment, expressing the child’s geometry in extrinsic, inherited coordinates.

**Conceptual Role**  

The observer frame is the *starting viewpoint* for every child layer.  
In this frame:

- \(Q_p\) is vertical  
- the child’s phase plane is generally tilted  
- the intrinsic Q‑axis \(Q_c\) is not yet vertical  
- polarisation is visible as a geometric misalignment  
- precession appears as a rotation of \(Q_c\) around \(Q_p\)  

The observer frame is not the child’s true geometry — it is the parent’s *interpretation* of it.  
Alignment rotates this frame so that \(Q_p\) coincides with \(Q_c\), revealing the child’s intrinsic structure.

**Geometric Expression**  
The observer’s coordinate triad:
$$
(x_p,\ k_p,\ Q_p)
$$
with \(Q_p\) inherited from the parent layer.

**Cross‑links**  
\(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Alignment, Polarisation, Frame Rotation, Intrinsic Geometry, Layer Inheritance.

**Interpretive Summary**  
The observer frame is the extrinsic viewpoint imposed by the parent layer.  
It is defined entirely by the inherited Q‑axis and therefore sees the child’s geometry in a skewed, tilted form.  
Only after alignment does the child’s intrinsic geometry become visible, with \(Q_c\) restored as the canonical vertical axis.



### **Observer Q‑Axis**

**Definition**  

The inherited Q‑axis \(Q_p\) as seen from the parent’s perspective.  
It is the vertical axis of the observer frame and the reference direction against which the child layer’s geometry is initially measured.

**Conceptual Role**  

The observer Q‑axis is the *parent’s vertical*.  
It determines:

- the orientation of the observer frame  
- how the child’s phase plane appears before alignment  
- the polarisation angle between \(Q_p\) and \(Q_c\)  
- the direction around which precession is observed  
- the axis that must be matched during alignment  

It is not generated by the child; it is imposed upon the child.

**Geometric Expression**  

The observer’s vertical direction:
$$
Q_{\text{obs}} = Q_p
$$
where \(Q_p\) is inherited from the parent layer.

**Cross‑links**  

Observer Frame, \(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Polarisation, Alignment, Layer Inheritance.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The observer Q‑axis is simply the inherited Q‑axis viewed from the parent’s frame.  
It defines the vertical direction of the observer frame and serves as the reference against which the child’s intrinsic Q‑axis is compared.  
All misalignment, polarisation, and precession are measured relative to this axis.



### **Orientation Sphere**

**Definition**  

The 2‑sphere that represents all possible orientations of the child’s intrinsic Q‑axis \(Q_c\) relative to the inherited Q‑axis \(Q_p\).  
Each point on the sphere corresponds to a unique tilt (polarisation) and azimuthal angle.

**Conceptual Role**  

The orientation sphere is the geometric space in which:

- polarisation is represented as a latitude (tilt angle)  
- azimuthal phase is represented as longitude  
- precession becomes motion along a line of latitude  
- alignment becomes a rotation that moves \(Q_c\) to the north pole  
- mass appears as the angular velocity of motion around the sphere  

It is the simplest, cleanest way to visualise the relationship between \(Q_p\) and \(Q_c\).

**Geometric Expression**  

The intrinsic Q‑axis \(Q_c\) is mapped to a point on:
$$
S^2_{\text{orient}} = \{\, \hat{n} \in \mathbb{R}^3 : |\hat{n}| = 1 \,\}
$$
with the inherited axis \(Q_p\) placed at the north pole.

**Cross‑links**  

Polarisation, Precession, \(Q_p\), \(Q_c\), Alignment, Mass, Observer Frame.

**Interpretive Summary**  

The orientation sphere is the geometric stage on which the drama of alignment, polarisation, and mass unfolds.  
It encodes the child’s Q‑axis as a point on a sphere, with tilt and azimuth determining how the child appears from the parent’s frame.  
Precession traces circles on this sphere; alignment moves the point to the pole; mass is the speed of that motion.



### **Orthogonality Conditions**

**Definition**

 The geometric constraints that ensure the phase plane, Q‑axis, and hyperspheres remain mutually orthogonal in the canonical frame.

**Conceptual Role**

 Orthogonality conditions guarantee:

- the phase plane is perpendicular to \(Q_c\)
- the x‑sphere and k‑sphere are cleanly separated
- the FT has no cross‑terms
- alignment produces a valid canonical frame

Without orthogonality, the dual‑sphere structure collapses.

**Geometric Expression**
$$
\langle e_1,Q𝚌\rangle =0,\quad \langle e_2,Q𝚌\rangle =0,\quad \langle e_1,e_2\rangle =0
$$


for the phase plane basis vectors \(e_1,e_2\).

**Cross‑links**
 Phase Plane, \(Q_c\), Canonical Axes, FT, Alignment.

**Interpretive Summary**
 Orthogonality conditions ensure that the phase plane, Q‑axis, and hyperspheres form a clean, perpendicular structure required for the FT.





### **Ortho‑Pairs **



![BasicPhase Circle](F:/Projects/Physics/Relativity/MyRelativity/figures/Diffraction chapter/BasicPhase Circle.png)

**Definition**  

An **ortho‑pair** is a pair of orthogonal fields whose instantaneous strengths are given by the projections of a **single rotating state vector**.  
The two fields are not independent: they are **two expressions of the same underlying rotation**, viewed along two perpendicular axes.  
As the state vector rotates, the fields **co‑oscillate**, continually exchanging energy.

This is the simplest dynamical unit in the OP‑framework.

---

**Interpretation of the Diagram**

- The rotating vector represents the **state** of the system.  
- The **x‑projection** gives the instantaneous strength of field X.  
- The **y‑projection** gives the instantaneous strength of field Y.  
- The sine and cosine waves show how the two fields **exchange energy** as the phase rotates.  
- The phase angle **φ** determines the **relative contribution** of each field at any moment.

This is the geometric origin of oscillation:  
**rotation in the phase plane becomes oscillation in the observable fields.**

---

**Examples**

**Electromagnetism**  

- x = Electric field **E**  
- y = Magnetic field **B**  
- E and B are orthogonal expressions of one rotating electromagnetic state.

**Orbital Mechanics**  

- x = Gravitational field strength **Q**  
- y = Kinetic field strength **v**  
- Q and v oscillate as energy moves between gravitational potential and kinetic motion.

---

**Beyond Pairs: Ortho‑Triples**

Some systems exhibit **three** fields that cyclically share energy.  
These are higher‑order generalisations of ortho‑pairs:

- **Quark colours** (cyclic SU(3) symmetry)  
- **Jupiter’s north‑pole hexagonal storm** (three interacting wave modes)

These are not the default structure of the OP‑framework, but they illustrate that the same geometric idea — **energy cycling through orthogonal modes** — can extend beyond pairs.
